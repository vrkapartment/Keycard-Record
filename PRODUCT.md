# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Dorm ("หอพัก") administrative staff who manage keycard records for tenant rooms. They work back-office, likely on a laptop during regular admin hours — not technical specialists, but comfortable with everyday web forms. The core job: look up a room, see its keycards and their status, register new cards, and move a card through the Active → Process Inactive → Inactive lifecycle when a tenant checks out or a card is lost/reissued.

## Product Purpose

Keycard Record replaces an ad-hoc paper/spreadsheet process for tracking the last-5-digit codes of dorm door keycards. It gives staff a single source of truth per room (a room can hold multiple cards over its lifetime), a clear 3-stage status lifecycle, and a queue of cards pending deactivation so nothing falls through the cracks. Success means staff trust the system enough to retire the parallel paper log.

## Brand Personality

Sharp, modern, professional, and trustworthy. Even though this is an internal operations tool (register: product), it should feel like premium software a well-run property management company would use — not a bare-bones internal script. Confident and precise rather than playful; the polish should reassure staff (and indirectly, tenants) that keycard security is handled competently.

## Anti-references

- Generic all-gray/zinc admin-template look (the current baseline) — flat, undifferentiated, no identity.
- Overly playful or consumer-app-cute treatments (this is a security/operations tool, not a lifestyle app).
- Cluttered enterprise-SaaS dashboards with dense chrome and low signal-to-noise.

## Design Principles

1. Status is the protagonist — Active / Process Inactive / Inactive must be instantly scannable at a glance, not just labeled text.
2. One committed accent color carries the brand; everything else stays quiet so the accent and status colors keep their meaning.
3. Every screen answers "what needs my attention right now" (e.g. the Process Inactive queue) before it answers "here's all the data."
4. Precision over decoration — confident type, purposeful whitespace, sharp and consistent detailing; no gratuitous ornament.
5. Thai-first typography and copy — hierarchy and spacing must hold up with Thai script line lengths and character shapes, not just Latin text.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA minimum: body text ≥4.5:1 contrast, status indicators never rely on color alone (pair with a text label), visible focus states on every interactive element, full keyboard navigation for forms and status-change controls.
